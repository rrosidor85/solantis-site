Coordinating Care With Your Siblings

By the Solantis Care Team · Updated June 2026 · 8 min read

When a parent needs care, the work rarely gets divided evenly — it lands on whoever is closest or says yes first. Here's how to share the load so it's fairer, calmer, and safer for everyone.

Of the 53 million Americans caring for a loved one, far too many are doing it largely alone — and research shows family caregivers' own health has gotten worse over the past several years. The hard part usually isn't a lack of willing siblings; it's the absence of a clear plan. A little structure turns "I thought you were handling Mom's refills" into a system everyone can rely on.

Step 1: Put every task on the table

Before dividing anything, name it. Caregiving is far more than driving to appointments. List the work across four buckets:

  • Medical: medications, appointments, talking to doctors, tracking symptoms
  • Financial & admin: bills, insurance, benefits, legal documents
  • Hands-on: meals, transportation, home safety, daily check-ins
  • Emotional: companionship, calls, being the person your parent confides in

Seeing the full list often reveals how much the primary caregiver is silently carrying — and where others can step in.

Step 2: Divide by strengths and logistics, not guilt

The fairest split isn't always equal — it's sensible. Match tasks to who's best positioned: the sibling who's good with money manages bills and benefits; the one who lives nearby handles in-person needs; the organized one owns the calendar and medication list. Decisions made from capability hold up; decisions made from guilt breed resentment.

Remember: A sibling 1,000 miles away can still carry real responsibility. Distance limits hands-on help, not ownership of finances, scheduling, or research.

Step 3: Choose one shared source of truth

The most common cause of family caregiving conflict is everyone working from different information. Pick one place where the current medication list, appointments, notes, and tasks live — and make sure everyone can see it. When the record is shared and current, "Did anyone refill her prescription?" stops being a guessing game. A shared care app with role-based access is purpose-built for this; a shared document is a workable start.

Step 4: Set a communication rhythm

Don't rely on crisis-driven phone calls. A short, regular check-in — even 15 minutes every couple of weeks — keeps everyone aligned and surfaces small problems before they grow. Decide how you'll handle urgent updates, too, so the local caregiver isn't the only one who ever hears bad news in real time.

Step 5: Give long-distance siblings real jobs

Remote family members often feel helpless and end up contributing little — or worse, second-guessing the on-the-ground caregiver. Hand them ownership of things distance doesn't affect:

  • Managing bills, insurance claims, and paperwork
  • Scheduling appointments and arranging transportation
  • Ordering refills and monitoring the medication list remotely
  • Researching benefits your parent may qualify for
  • Being the documented point of contact for a provider or two

Step 6: Protect the primary caregiver

Whoever does the most is at the highest risk of burnout — which ultimately hurts your parent too. Build in respite: scheduled breaks, rotating a duty, or bringing in paid help for specific tasks. Watch for signs of exhaustion and treat the primary caregiver's wellbeing as part of the care plan, not an afterthought.

Sharing care well doesn't require a perfect family. It requires a clear list, honest roles, and one place everyone can see the truth. Get those three right and the rest gets a lot more manageable.

Frequently asked questions

How do siblings divide caregiving responsibilities fairly?

List every task — medical, financial, hands-on, and emotional — then assign based on each person's strengths, skills, and proximity rather than guilt. Remote siblings can own finances, scheduling, insurance, and research; local siblings handle in-person needs.

How can long-distance siblings help with an aging parent?

Plenty, without being present: managing bills and insurance, scheduling appointments, ordering refills, researching benefits, and serving as a provider point of contact. A shared care app lets remote siblings stay fully informed and carry real responsibility.

How do we avoid conflict when caring for a parent together?

Agree on one shared source of truth for medications, appointments, and notes, set a regular check-in, and make decisions transparently. Most family conflict comes from unequal load and information gaps — both of which shared tools reduce.
